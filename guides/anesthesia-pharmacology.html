Anesthetic agents work by interacting with various receptors in the brain and body to produce unconsciousness, analgesia, and muscle relaxation.

2. Inhalational Anesthetics

     O    O    O
      \  |  /
       \ | /
    ----( )----  Alveoli
       / | \
      /  |  \
     O    O    O

Inhalational anesthetics such as sevoflurane, desflurane, and isoflurane are administered via the lungs and act primarily on the central nervous system.

  • Sevoflurane: Fast onset and offset, sweet smell
  • Desflurane: Very fast onset and offset, pungent smell
  • Isoflurane: Slower onset and offset, pungent smell

Intravenous anesthetics like propofol, etomidate, and ketamine are administered directly into the bloodstream for rapid onset of action.

  • Propofol: Rapid onset and offset, antiemetic properties
  • Etomidate: Minimal cardiovascular effects, used in hemodynamically unstable patients
  • Ketamine: Dissociative anesthetic, provides analgesia

Opioids are used in anesthesia for their analgesic properties and to reduce the required dose of other anesthetic agents.

  • Fentanyl: Potent, short-acting
  • Remifentanil: Ultra-short-acting, rapid offset
  • Morphine: Longer-acting, used for postoperative pain

Neuromuscular blocking agents paralyze skeletal muscles to facilitate intubation and surgical conditions.

  • Rocuronium: Non-depolarizing, intermediate duration
  • Succinylcholine: Depolarizing, ultra-short duration
  • Cisatracurium: Non-depolarizing, organ-independent elimination
